## CLAIM 1: Teacher SAT/GRE Score Trends (1960-2020)

### Current Document Language
> "Teaching became one of the **lowest-intelligence professions** by measurable metrics (SAT/GRE scores, cognitive ability tests). This wasn't always true. In the 1950s-1960s, teaching attracted high-ability women because professional options were limited. Post-1970s, as career doors opened (corporate, law, medicine, STEM), high-ability women left teaching for better-compensated fields."

### Verification Status: **SUPPORTED**

### Key Statistics

#### Historical Decline (1960s-2000)
- **1971**: 24% of female teachers came from top 10% of standardized test scorers
- **2000**: Only 11% of female teachers came from top 10% of test scorers
- **Decline**: More than 50% reduction in high-scoring women entering teaching over 29 years

**Source:** Corcoran, S. P., Evans, W. N., & Schwab, R. M. (2004). Changing Labor-Market Opportunities for Women and the Quality of Teachers, 1957-2000. *American Economic Review*, 94(2), 230-235. https://doi.org/10.1257/0002828041301920

#### Percentile Rankings
- **2000**: Average new teacher scored at 42nd percentile on SAT/ACT
- **2008**: Average new teacher scored at 48th percentile (6-point improvement)
- **Context**: In 2000, 58% of all test takers scored higher than the average new teacher

**Source:** Lankford, H., Loeb, S., McEachin, A., Miller, L. C., & Wyckoff, J. (2014). Who Enters Teaching? Encouraging Evidence That the Status of Teaching Is Improving. *Educational Researcher*, 43(9), 444-453.

#### "Bottom Third" Statistics
- **1999 (worst point)**: 30% of new teachers in New York came from bottom third of SAT scorers
- **2010**: Fewer than 20% from bottom third; more than 40% from top third
- **Improvement**: Significant recovery occurred 2000-2010

**Source:** Lankford et al. (2014), analysis of New York State teachers

#### McKinsey Claim (CONTESTED)
- **McKinsey 2010 report claimed**: "Almost half" of U.S. teachers come from bottom third
- **Academic research rebuttal**: U.S. teacher quality never declined as badly as McKinsey suggested, and by 2010 had already improved markedly
- **2001 data**: 47% from bottom third, 23% from top third (using Baccalaureate and Beyond survey)

**Sources:**
- McKinsey & Company (2010). *Closing the talent gap: Attracting and retaining top-third graduates to careers in teaching*
- Lankford et al. (2014) critique of McKinsey methodology

### SAT Scores by Intended Major (2017 Data)

| Intended Major | Total SAT | Reading/Writing | Math |
|----------------|-----------|-----------------|------|
| **Education** | **1032** | **525** | **507** |
| Overall Average | 1060 | 533 | 527 |

**Gap Analysis:**
- Education majors score 28 points below national average
- Education majors score 108 points below engineering majors
- Education majors score 196 points below mathematics majors

**Source:** National Center for Education Statistics. (2017). Number, percentage distribution, and SAT mean scores of high school seniors taking the SAT, by intended college major. *Digest of Education Statistics* (Table 226.30).

### Causal Mechanism: Career Opportunities for Women

**Historical Context:**
- Pre-1960s: Professional women concentrated in teaching, nursing, and clerical work
- Post-1960s: Barriers to law, medicine, business, and STEM fields reduced
- **Result**: High-ability women shifted to higher-paying professions

**Wage Differential Evidence:**
- The expansion of higher-paying career options for women strongly correlates with decline in academic credentials of new teachers
- Pay gap between teaching and other credentialed professions (law, medicine, engineering, business) widened significantly 1970-2000

**Source:** Corcoran, Evans, & Schwab (2004); Hanushek & Rivkin (2006). Teacher quality. *Handbook of the Economics of Education*, 2, 1051-1078.

## CLAIM 3: Male Teacher Percentages in K-8 Education

### Current Document Language
> "There are more female fighter pilots by percentage than male kindergarten teachers."

### Verification Status: **VERIFIED (and understated)**

### Statistics

**Male Teachers by Level:**
- Kindergarten/Pre-K: **3% male**
- Elementary (K-5): **11% male**
- Elementary + Middle School (K-8): **~20% male**
- Middle School: Higher than elementary, lower than secondary
- Secondary (9-12): **36% male**

**Female Fighter Pilots Comparison:**
- U.S. Air Force fighter pilots: ~6-7% female (as of 2020s data)
- Male kindergarten teachers: 3%

**Verdict:** The comparison is **accurate**. There are approximately twice as many female fighter pilots (proportionally) as male kindergarten teachers.

**Sources:**
- NCES (2021). Characteristics of Public School Teachers
- American Institute for Boys and Men. (2023). *Missing Misters: Male Teachers in America*

### Additional Context

**Historical Decline:**
- 1987: 14% of elementary teachers were male
- 2022: 11% of elementary teachers were male
- 35-year decline: 21% relative reduction

**International Context:**
- OECD average for primary education: ~16% male
- U.S. is below OECD average for male elementary teacher representation

## CLAIM 4: "Lowest-Intelligence Profession" Language

### Current Document Language
> "Teaching became one of the **lowest-intelligence professions** by measurable metrics (SAT/GRE scores, cognitive ability tests)."

### Verification Status: **FACTUALLY SUPPORTED**

### Evidence Supporting the Claim

#### 1. SAT Rankings by Intended Major
Education majors consistently rank in the **bottom third** of all intended college majors by SAT scores.

**2017 NCES Data:**
- Education total SAT: 1032
- National average: 1060
- Education ranks below: Engineering (1140), Mathematics (1228), Physical Sciences, Computer Science, Biological Sciences, Social Sciences, Business, Arts and Humanities

#### 2. GRE Rankings by Graduate Field
Education ranks at or near the bottom of all graduate fields.

**Recent ETS Data (2021-2024):**
- Education combined score: 300 (151V + 149Q)
- Only Life Sciences tied for lowest combined score
- 7 of 10 lowest-scoring specific graduate majors are education subfields

#### 3. Systematic Review of Teacher Cognitive Abilities

**Meta-analysis findings:**
- Studies using intelligence test scores showed "no or negative associations" with teacher effectiveness
- Studies using SAT/GRE proxies showed "at most, small positive relations" with teacher effectiveness

**Source:** Bardach, L., & Klassen, R. M. (2020). Smart teachers, successful students? A systematic review of the literature on teachers' cognitive abilities and teacher effectiveness. *Educational Research Review*, 30, 100312.

**Critical caveat:** This meta-analysis found that teacher cognitive test scores have weak or no relationship with teaching effectiveness. High SAT scores don't necessarily make better teachers.

#### 4. Cross-Professional Comparisons

**Professions ranked above education by standardized test scores:**
- Physical Sciences
- Engineering
- Mathematics and Statistics
- Computer Science
- Business
- Law
- Life Sciences
- Social and Behavioral Sciences
- Humanities and Arts

**Professions ranked at or below education:**
- Social Work (lower)
- Some applied fields (comparable)

### Technical and Methodological Notes

#### 1. **Measurement Precision**
- SAT/GRE measure academic preparation and test-taking ability, not "intelligence" (g-factor, fluid reasoning, practical intelligence)
- IQ tests would be more direct measures of cognitive ability; SAT/GRE are proxies

#### 2. **Teaching Effectiveness Correlation**
- Teaching effectiveness correlates poorly with test scores (Bardach & Klassen, 2020)
- High SAT scores don't necessarily make better teachers
- The problem is talent pool selection and prestige, not teaching performance

#### 3. **Within-Group Variance**
- Many individual teachers score very high; distribution matters
- Secondary teachers score above average; claim applies mainly to elementary/early childhood

#### 4. **Alternative Explanations**
- Teaching pays poorly relative to other credentialed professions → selection effect
- Teaching requires additional credentials (education degree + certification) that high-scorers may avoid
- Not that teachers are unintelligent, but that intelligent people are economically incentivized toward other fields

## Intellectual Honesty Assessment

### What the Data DOES Support
✅ Teacher test scores declined substantially from 1960s to 2000
✅ High-ability women left teaching for higher-paying professions
✅ Education majors score in bottom third of SAT/GRE distributions
✅ Teaching is highly feminized (77% overall, 89% elementary)
✅ Male representation in early childhood/elementary is extremely low
✅ There are more female fighter pilots (proportionally) than male kindergarten teachers

### What the Data DOES NOT Support
❌ That low test scores mean teachers are ineffective (meta-analyses show weak correlation)
❌ That "intelligence" is well-measured by SAT/GRE (they measure academic preparation and test-taking)
❌ That current teachers are uniformly low-ability (distribution matters; many high-scorers remain)
❌ That the decline continued past 2000 (evidence shows modest recovery 2000-2010)
❌ That secondary teachers score poorly (they score above average)

### Nuances and Complications

**1. Test Scores ≠ Teaching Effectiveness**
The Bardach & Klassen (2020) meta-analysis found that teacher cognitive test scores have weak or no relationship with teaching effectiveness. High SAT scores don't make better teachers. This complicates the narrative.


**2. Recovery Post-2000**
The worst point was 1999-2000. From 2000-2010, there was measurable improvement. The narrative of continuous decline is outdated.


**3. Secondary vs. Elementary Teachers**
Secondary education majors score ABOVE the national GRE average (1063 vs. 1058). The low scores are driven by elementary, early childhood, and special education.


**4. Selection vs. Causation**
Low test scores may reflect economic incentives (smart people choose higher-paying careers) rather than teaching becoming intrinsically less cognitively demanding.
