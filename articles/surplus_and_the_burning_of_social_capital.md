# Surplus and the Burning of Social Capital

*[Skeleton draft — argument first, polish after]*

---

## I. The Observation

Balaji Srinivasan has noted that golden ages burn the social capital that created them. This is not obvious. Prosperity should reinforce itself — more resources mean more capacity to invest in the institutions and norms that produced the prosperity in the first place. And yet the pattern recurs: Rome, the American postwar era, Japan's economic miracle, the British Empire at its height. The golden age generates the conditions of its own decay.

The question is why. Not in the hand-wavy sense of "success breeds complacency" — that's a description, not a mechanism. There is a specific causal chain, and it runs through surplus.

---

## II. What Surplus Actually Does

Surplus is not just "more stuff." It is the condition of having more resources than subsistence requires. When surplus exists, people can be freed from the farm — from the immediate, inescapable feedback loop of produce or starve. More people can be directed toward other uses.

Some of those other uses are productive: art, science, administration, trade, military capacity. These are the visible face of civilization. But surplus does not select for productive use. It creates possibility space. And among the possibilities it creates is extraction.

The critical insight is arithmetic. When resources are scarce, the extractive class is small, because there is only so much surplus to sustain parasites. When surplus grows, it changes who can be funded. Not just the extractive elite at the top, but layers beneath them: bureaucratic empires, administrative classes, coalition allies, ideological enforcers. The incentive to corrupt a system scales with the resources available to be captured. A foreign actor pours money into US politics because the returns on that investment are enormous. They do not do the same in Uruguay, not because Uruguay is more virtuous but because the prize is smaller.

This is the first thing surplus does: it expands the feasible size of the parasitic layer.

---

## III. The Binding Problem

The question is never whether surplus creates extraction opportunities. It always does. The question is whether a society's institutions and social capital are strong enough to bind it.

Think of two forces in tension:

**Binding force**: social capital, cultural norms, accountability structures, the proximity of consequences — the degree to which defection is visible and punished.

**Unbinding force**: the amount of surplus × the scale of the system × the form the surplus takes.

When binding force exceeds unbinding force, surplus gets channeled into productive use. The society builds. When unbinding force exceeds binding force, extraction runs away. And here is the self-reinforcing part: unbound surplus does not merely fund extraction. It funds the erosion of the institutions that could have bound it. The parasitic layer, once established, has strong incentives to dismantle accountability structures, capture regulatory agencies, and reshape cultural norms in ways that legitimize extraction. The binds themselves get consumed.

This is why Balaji's observation is precise. The golden age doesn't just suffer decay — it actively funds it.

---

## IV. Form and Scale Are Not Incidental

Not all surplus is equally dangerous. The form surplus takes and the scale at which it operates determine how hard it is to bind.

**Form**: Land is hard to hide, hard to move, and easy to tax. It stays within a community and its ownership is legible. Liquid financial capital is the opposite — it moves across borders instantaneously, hides in shell structures, and corrupts at distance. Post-1970 America removed the restrictions that had kept capital relatively immobile and productive-asset-adjacent. What replaced it was a form of surplus specifically resistant to the binding mechanisms that had contained earlier forms.

**Scale**: Corruption is harder to sustain at local scale not because local politicians are more ethical, but because social distance is shorter. A county commissioner who embezzles is likely to encounter his victims at the grocery store. A federal official who captures a regulatory agency never meets the people harmed. Scale provides anonymity. And beyond anonymity, scale creates incentive: the prize for corrupting a national institution is orders of magnitude larger than corrupting a local one.

This is the Dunbar number problem applied to governance. Human social accountability evolved for groups of roughly 150 people. Above that threshold, the feedback mechanisms that punish defection become progressively weaker. National-scale institutions operate at hundreds or thousands of times that limit. The binds that work at human scale do not automatically transfer.

This also explains the apparent virtue of small countries. Uruguay and the Nordic nations are not more honest cultures in some deep sense. They are operating at a scale where the ratio between binding force and unbinding force is more favorable: less surplus to capture, shorter social distances, harder to hide. The binding mechanisms don't have to be as strong because the unbinding force is weaker.

## V. The Downstream Effects

Surplus being unbound does not produce one kind of dysfunction. It produces several, operating simultaneously at different levels. These are not the same mechanism — they are different-level effects of the same enabling condition.

### The Political-Economic Level

At the institutional level, surplus enables the growth of unaccountable bureaucratic classes. This is partly a story about incentives — more surplus means larger prizes for capture — but it is also a story about organizational logic. Bureaucracies grow by internal pressure independent of their external function. The number of people reporting to a manager is a proxy for that manager's importance and salary. Every manager in a bureaucracy has personal incentives to expand their headcount regardless of what the headcount produces. Parkinson's Law is not a joke: work expands to fill the time available, and bureaucracies expand to consume the resources allocated to them.

Once established, these classes are self-perpetuating. Their political mass makes them hard to dislodge. They develop ideological justifications for their existence. They capture the oversight mechanisms designed to constrain them. And they operate at a scale where Dunbar-proximity accountability does not reach.

### The Biological Level

The deeper mechanism, and the more parsimonious explanation for a range of civilizational pathologies, comes from Dani Sulikowski's research on intrasexual competition (ISC) — competition between members of the same sex for reproductive success.

In females, ISC operates primarily through suppressing rivals' fertility rather than directly maximizing one's own. When hierarchies become saturated with competitors, this mechanism activates: stress responses suppress reproduction, cooperation radii contract, and individuals who recognize they cannot reproduce shift toward harming rival lineages — kin-selected spite — rather than withdrawing.

Surplus is what enables competitive saturation at scale. When surplus frees people from subsistence, they flow into cities, into institutions, into hierarchies. The density of competition increases. The ISC mechanism, calibrated for the scale of a clan or village, gets applied to institutions of thousands or millions.

At institutional scale, ISC manifests as: removal of meritocratic barriers (expanding access to institutional position, which is itself surplus), enforcement of conformity and harmony norms over functional ones, reputation-based exclusion ratchets, and mission displacement — converting function-oriented institutions into belonging-oriented ones. Sulikowski's research documents a measurable signature: women consistently give more reproductively inhibiting advice to others than they report they would follow themselves. The gap between self-prescription and advice-to-others is ISC operating through social norms.

These behaviors are adaptive at the lineage level. Suppressing rivals' reproduction while maintaining one's own is a winning strategy in a saturated hierarchy. The civilizational destruction is emergent — the aggregate of many locally rational moves, none of which was designed to destroy the institution.

This is not an argument about malice. It is an argument about selection pressure. Surplus creates the conditions where these programs activate. The programs then run.

---

## VI. The Self-Reinforcing Loop

Each of these mechanisms — bureaucratic empire building, coalition corruption, ISC at institutional scale — shares a common feature: once running, they generate pressure to continue running.

The bureaucratic class has incentives to capture the institutions that could constrain it. The extractive coalition has incentives to reshape the rules that defined extraction as illegitimate. The ISC dynamic, once it has sufficiently lowered institutional quality, produces institutions that select for the traits that sustain it.

And all of them are funded by the surplus. The golden age is not merely being spent on extraction — it is being spent on eroding the capacity to resist extraction. Balaji's observation is precise: the social capital burns.

---

## VII. The Counterfactual

The theory would be unfalsifiable if every society eventually decayed. Some don't — or at least, some maintain coherence far longer under surplus than the mechanism would predict. Understanding why completes the argument.

Post-WWII Japan ran the full cycle within living memory. MacArthur's occupation redistributed ownership — not income, which requires ongoing political will and is constantly eroded, but *ownership*, which restructures the incentive layer directly. The keiretsu competed and invested because their success depended on output, not extraction. The miracle followed. Then, over decades, the iron triangle of bureaucrats, politicians, and business leaders consolidated. The Plaza Accord forced monetary easing. The synthetic surplus of asset inflation replaced productive surplus. The binds eroded. The Lost Decades followed.

The Nordic countries maintained their binding force longer not through superior culture but through institutional structures that made decoupling mechanically harder: codetermination laws (workers on corporate boards by statute, not by goodwill), public salary transparency (defection is visible), and cooperative ownership structures that kept the incentive layer distributed. And they operated at scale where Dunbar-proximity accountability still partially functions.

The pattern in each case is the same: what varies is the ratio. Binding force relative to unbinding force. Strong enough institutions relative to the size and form and scale of the surplus they are trying to contain.

---

## VIII. What This Means

None of these mechanisms require bad actors in the cartoon sense. The bureaucrat expanding his department is responding rationally to incentives. The extractive coalition funding political access is maximizing returns. The ISC dynamic is adaptive at the level of the lineage running it. The emergent destruction is real; the malice is not required.

This matters for diagnosis. If the decay were caused by villains, removing the villains would fix it. The actual cause is structural: surplus above a threshold, in a liquid form, at a scale that exceeds the binding capacity of existing institutions. The fix is not moral — it is architectural. Either strengthen the binds (which is extraordinarily hard when the surplus is already funding their erosion) or reduce the scale and form of surplus accessible to extraction (which is also hard, and politically resisted by everyone benefiting from the current arrangement).

Post-1970 America removed the restrictions on capital mobility at the precise moment when the surplus was at its historical peak and the institutional binding mechanisms were still calibrated for an earlier, more legible form of wealth. The unbinding force grew. The binding force did not. The rest follows.

The diagnostic question for any society is not "are your institutions good?" It is: is your binding force keeping pace with the surplus it is trying to contain? And if the surplus is already running — if the golden age is already burning — the harder question is whether anything can be rebuilt faster than it is being consumed.
